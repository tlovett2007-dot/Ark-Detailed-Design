Moon Pool

Cubit: 0.524m (Royal Egyptian)  |  Last revised: July 2026  |  Status: Working document
Home › Moon Pool

The moon pool is an opening through the hull bottom, open to the sea, used for waste disposal, bilge access, and drainage. It is a structural feature requiring careful engineering — a deliberate hole in the hull of a vessel in a global flood demands serious design justification. The volume tally deducts 728 m³ for the "double moon pool complex."

1. What is a Moon Pool?

A moon pool (also known as a well or trunk) is a vertical shaft through the hull of a vessel, open at the bottom to the sea and enclosed within the hull structure above the waterline. The water level inside the moon pool equalises with the sea — it does not flood the vessel because the surrounding hull structure and the air pressure above keep water from rising above the waterline level.

PropertyDetail
Water level insideEqualises with external sea level — rises and falls with waves
Flooding riskMinimal — air pressure above prevents water rising above sea level. Same principle as an inverted glass in water.
Wave actionWater oscillates in the pool with vessel motion — requires baffling for severe conditions
Historical useFishing vessels, offshore platforms, research vessels — well established technology
Waste disposalWaste dropped into pool drains directly to sea by gravity and wave action
Why a moon pool and not a drain valve? A valve is a failure point — a stuck closed valve blocks drainage; a stuck open valve floods the vessel. The moon pool has no moving parts — waste falls through by gravity, sea water self-equalises, no crew operation required under normal conditions. Consistent with the passive systems design philosophy throughout. See Passive Systems.

2. The Double Moon Pool Complex

The volume tally specifies a "double moon pool complex" — two moon pools rather than one. The rationale for doubling:

ReasonDetail
RedundancyIf one pool becomes blocked (debris, ice, biological growth over 371 days) the other continues functioning
Separation of functionsOne pool for waste disposal (Zone 3 large animal drainage + Zone 2 cleanout disposal); one pool for bilge access and general drainage
Wave dynamicsTwo pools can be baffled to reduce resonant sloshing — a single large pool in severe conditions could develop dangerous oscillation
Location flexibilityWaste pool positioned adjacent to Zone 3 large animals (short sewer run); bilge pool positioned for structural access

3. Location

Moon pool location is driven by three competing requirements:

RequirementPreferred location
Zone 3 waste drainage — short sewer runDirectly below Zone 3 large animal area — top deck, requires vertical shaft through all three decks
Structural integrityAway from maximum bending moment zone (amidships) — toward bow or stern third
Wave dynamicsAway from extreme ends — slamming and pitching most severe at bow and stern
Bilge accessNear ballast zone — allows inspection of ballast tank condition

The competing requirements suggest moon pool location in the after third of the vessel — away from the amidships maximum bending zone, away from bow slamming, close enough to stern for Zone 3 sewer run, and adjacent to the stern service compartment for crew access and management.

4. Structural Design

The Hull Opening Problem

A moon pool is a discontinuity in the hull — a stress concentration in the primary structural member (the hull skin box girder). At 155m+ length with fully reversing wave loads, any discontinuity requires careful reinforcement.

Structural challengeSolution
Longitudinal bending interrupted by pool openingHeavy ring frame around pool perimeter — restores continuity of the box girder flanges
Stress concentration at cornersRadiused corners — no sharp re-entrant angles in the pool perimeter
Water load on pool wallsPool trunk is a tube under external hydrostatic pressure — wall thickness designed accordingly
Wave sloshing loadsBaffles within pool reduce oscillation amplitude — prevents resonant sloshing in heavy weather

Pool Trunk Construction

The moon pool trunk runs from the hull bottom through all three decks to the weatherdeck level — a vertical shaft through the entire vessel height. Construction:

ElementSpecification
Trunk wallsHeavy laminated timber — structural continuity with hull skin
Deck penetrationsHeavy ring frames at each deck level — redistributes loads around opening
Pool bottomOpen to sea — no closing mechanism required
Pool topGrating at weatherdeck level — prevents accidental entry, allows waste disposal
BafflesTimber baffle plates reducing pool cross-section by ~50% — damps oscillation
WaterproofingFull pitch treatment of all surfaces — prevents rot in continuously wet environment

5. Waste Disposal Functions

Zone 3 Daily Drainage

The primary waste disposal function. Large animal wash-down water and solid waste flows by gravity from Zone 3 slatted floors through a short sewer run (few metres horizontally) to the moon pool trunk, then falls to sea level and drains through the open bottom.

Zone 3 daily waste flow: 500 large animals × ~5kg waste/day = 2,500kg/day solid waste Wash-down water: estimated ~5 m³/day Total flow: ~7.5 m³/day = ~87mL/second continuous Short sewer run at 1% gradient: Flow velocity: adequate for self-cleaning Pipe diameter: ~200mm sufficient for this flow rate Blockage risk: low with daily flush cycle

Zone 2 Bi-Monthly Disposal

Every 60 days, Zone 2 litter (~10 m³ per cleanout) is barrowed to the moon pool zone and tipped into the pool. Wave action in the pool disperses the material to sea over subsequent days. The pool volume (~280 m³ per pool) is sufficient to contain a full cleanout load temporarily while wave action disperses it.

Zone 1 Bi-Monthly Disposal

Zone 1 desiccated litter (~1.5 m³ per cleanout) — same disposal route as Zone 2. Much smaller volume — easily managed.

Bilge Access and Inspection

The second moon pool (bilge pool) provides access to the ballast zone below Deck 1 — allowing inspection of:

Tilt-weir pump trough conditionMonthly — pitched fabric integrity
Ballast cell baffle conditionQuarterly

Sea Water Access

The moon pool provides controlled access to sea water for:

UseNotes
Zone 3 wash-down supplementationSalt water acceptable for floor wash — conserves fresh water
Fire suppressionPitch cooker fire risk — sea water immediately available at pool
Hull exterior observationLimited visibility through pool — confirms no major external damage
Sea water quality during flood: During the early flood phase (days 1–150) the sea water is a complex mix of ocean water, groundwater from "fountains of the deep," sediment and organic material. Quality for wash-down purposes may be poor. Fresh water from header tanks may be preferable for Zone 3 wash-down during this phase. Sea water quality improves as the flood stabilises — by the receding phase (days 150–371) it approaches normal ocean salinity.

7. Wave Dynamics in the Pool

In heavy weather the moon pool water surface oscillates with vessel motion. If the pool's natural resonant frequency matches the wave encounter frequency, dangerous resonant sloshing can develop — water sloshing violently in the pool trunk, potentially damaging surrounding structure and ejecting water onto working decks.

Moon pool natural frequency estimate: For a simple rectangular pool of width w and depth h: Natural frequency ≈ √(g × π / (2 × h)) / (2π) At depth h = 7.86m (draft): f_natural ≈ √(9.81 × π / (2 × 7.86)) / (2π) ≈ √(1.96) / 6.28 ≈ 1.4 / 6.28 ≈ 0.22 Hz = period ~4.5 seconds Design wave encounter period: ~14–22 seconds Pool natural period: ~4.5 seconds Ratio: ~3–5 — well away from resonance ✓ Baffles reduce effective pool area → increase natural frequency further → moves further from wave encounter period ✓

The moon pool natural period (~4.5 seconds) is well removed from the design wave encounter period (14–22 seconds) — resonance is not a concern at normal sea states. Baffles provide additional margin.
