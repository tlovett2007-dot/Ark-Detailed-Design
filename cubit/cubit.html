This page documents the cubit selection for this design, the reasoning behind choosing the Royal Egyptian Cubit (REC) of 524mm, and the reconciliation with the Ark Encounter (AiG) dimensions based on the Nippur cubit of 518.2mm. The argument presented here supersedes the author's 2006 recommendation of 518.5mm (Lovett, Journal of Creation 20(3):71–77, 2006), with additional reasoning from detailed design work not available at that time.

1. Background — The 2006 Paper

Lovett (2006) argued that the royal cubit class (517–546mm) is the correct choice for Noah's Ark on the following grounds:

The 2006 paper recommended 518.5mm (Nippur cubit) as a slightly conservative choice within the royal cubit class, primarily for geographic reasons — the Nippur rod is Mesopotamian, closer to the post-flood world origin than Egypt.

2. Why 518.5mm is Not a Real Number

The Nippur cubit rod is a copper bar dated to approximately 1950 BC, now in the Archaeological Museum of Istanbul. It is the oldest surviving physical length standard. However its reliability as a precise standard is limited:

ProblemDetail
Single irregular artefactThe bar is irregularly shaped and marked. The 518.5mm figure is one scholar's measurement of an imperfect object.
Contradicted by contemporary evidenceDoes not agree with other evidence from the statues of Gudea from the same region, five centuries later.
Possible ceremonial artefactRecent scholarship suggests the Nippur rod may have been a symbolic temple artefact rather than a working measuring standard — dimensions may be idealised.
Post-Babel dateDated ~1950 BC — approximately 400 years after the flood and Babel dispersion. Already a degraded copy of whatever Noah's original standard was.
Not a defined standard518.5mm is a measured artefact dimension, not a defined length. You cannot build to a measurement of a worn copper rod.
Lovett (2006) acknowledged"The general picture is that cubits outside of Egypt were less exact." — the author's own footnote 19.
The fundamental problem: 518.6mm (as sometimes quoted) is not a real number — it is a measurement of a worn irregular artefact. A design standard requires a defined value. 518.5mm is therefore unsuitable as a precise cubit specification for detailed engineering calculations.

3. Why 524mm (REC) is the Correct Choice

The Royal Egyptian Cubit (REC) at 524mm is the best-attested ancient length standard in existence:

EvidenceDetail
Multiple physical rodsPetrie (1877): "about a dozen of the actual cubit rods that are known yield 20.65 ± 0.01 in" — extraordinary precision for ancient metrology. A value still considered accurate today.
Architectural confirmationConfirmed independently from thousands of architectural measurements across centuries — pyramids of Giza, temples, palaces.
Internal consistencyConsistent across different dynasties and sites spanning thousands of years — varied less than 5% over its entire history of use.
Legally enforced precisionEgyptian building overseers required the REC to be calibrated against a precision standard at regular intervals. Failure was punishable by death — the most rigorously maintained ancient standard known.
Defined subdivision7 palms × 4 digits = 28 digits — a precisely defined internal structure, not just a measured length.
Babel dispersion candidateEgypt received post-Babel migrants earliest and built most prolifically — the best-preserved version of whatever cubit dispersed from Babel.
Selected cubit: 524mm (Royal Egyptian Cubit)
Measurement convention: outside of structural planking (moulded + structural skin)
This supersedes the 518.5mm Nippur recommendation of Lovett (2006).

5. Reconciliation with Ark Encounter (AiG)

The Ark Encounter uses 518.2mm (20.4 inches, described as "Nippur cubit") giving dimensions of 155.45m × 25.91m × 15.54m. This appears to contradict the REC dimensions of 157.2m × 26.2m × 15.72m. The apparent contradiction is resolved by measurement convention and construction specification — both describe the same physical vessel measured differently.

Fig 1. Ark Encounter under construction — laminated curved ribs define the outer form. The thin cladding visible on the right is effectively weatherproofing only, not structural planking. The AiG dimensions are therefore moulded dimensions (outside of structural ribs) by definition.

Length Reconciliation

The Ark Encounter was built to the author's CAD model as the outer skin. Their laminated rib structure defines the moulded form — the thin external cladding (~25mm) is not structural planking. Therefore:

The 1.436m difference is accounted for by 4 layers of 5" structural planking at the bow and stern, where planking runs at approximately 45° to the centreline:

Planking contribution to length (both ends): 4 layers × 127mm × 1.414 (45° factor) × 2 ends = 4 × 127 × 1.414 × 2 = 1,436mm = 1.436m ✓ AiG moulded (Nippur): 155.45m + structural planking: + 1.436m = Outside planking (REC): 156.886m ≈ 157.2m ✓
The length reconciliation is exact: Nippur moulded = REC outside structural planking, with the difference accounted for entirely by 4 layers of 5" structural planking at the bow/stern 45° planking angle. No special pleading required.

Beam — The Promenade Argument

The beam comparison is more complex due to the fundamentally different hull skin specifications between a museum exhibit and a seagoing vessel:

Moulded beamHull skin each sideInterior beamPromenadesCage width
Ark Encounter25.91m~0.25m~25.41m3 × ~3m = 9m~16.4m

The Ark Encounter's wider interior (25.41m vs 23.1m) is consumed by three full-length longitudinal public access corridors — required for visitor flow, fire egress, and wheelchair compliance with building codes. These are legitimate museum requirements that Noah did not face with eight crew members.

Noah's design uses a single 4m promenade simultaneously serving all operational functions: animal welfare rounds, ventilation shaft, bird accommodation, sprouting garden, and water distribution inspection corridor. The result is that Noah's narrower interior delivers more usable animal accommodation width (19.1m vs 16.4m) than the Ark Encounter's wider interior — because one functional promenade outperforms three public access corridors in space efficiency.

The beam reconciliation summary: The Ark Encounter's extra interior beam width is entirely consumed by public access infrastructure requirements absent from Noah's design. Noah's single promenade (4m) vs AiG's three corridors (~9m total) accounts for the interior beam difference, while delivering more usable animal accommodation space. This is a design efficiency difference, not a dimensional inconsistency.

7. AiG Ark Encounter — Cubit Methodology Note

The Ark Encounter describes their cubit derivation as: "Beginning with one of the shortest common cubits of 17.5 inches, we added a handbreadth of 2.9 inches, yielding a royal cubit length of 20.4 inches" (Ark Encounter blog, 2024, no individual author credited).

This methodology — deriving a royal cubit by adding a handbreadth to the Siloam tunnel Hebrew common cubit — is the same approach that Lovett (2006) specifically argued against, noting that the Siloam tunnel cubit post-dates Noah by approximately 2,000 years and has no connection to the antediluvian world. The AiG derivation arrives at approximately the correct number (~518mm) by a questionable route.

The REC at 524mm, derived directly from Petrie's measurements of actual ancient cubit rods (±0.01 inches precision), is the more rigorously evidenced choice — and as demonstrated above, is consistent with the AiG Ark Encounter dimensions when measurement conventions are properly applied.
