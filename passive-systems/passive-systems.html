Passive Systems

Home › Passive Systems

The name Noah (Hebrew: nûaḥ) means rest or comfort — to be at ease. The Ark's design reflects this: every major operational requirement is met by a self-regulating passive system requiring minimal or no crew intervention. Eight people managing 6,000 animals for 371 days is only viable if the vessel largely runs itself.

Complete Passive Systems Register

SystemMechanismCrew operationBenefit
Weathervaning Bow/stern hull asymmetry — bow presents less resistance, stern digs in. Vessel naturally aligns bow-into-wind under any wind load. None Keeps bow into steep local wind sea — reduces roll, reduces boarding seas, reduces structural loads
Rainwater harvesting Roof slope drains to outboard parapet gutter. Self-regulating flap valves open in calm/rain, close under wave pressure (green water). Gravity feed to corner header tanks. None after initial valve setting Replenishes header tanks throughout wet phase (days 1–150) without crew effort
Header tank gravity distribution Corner tanks at roof level gravity-feed down through outboard service column to cage-level water troughs at each deck. None Animal drinking water maintained without pumping or crew involvement
Tilt-weir pump Series of open timber troughs with incrementally higher overflow walls, zigzagging from ballast zone to roof level. Vessel pitch/roll tilts water surface over overflow walls — ratchets water upward one step per tilt cycle. No valves, no moving parts. None — operates whenever vessel moves in seaway Replenishes header tanks from bulk ballast during dry phase (days 150–371) without pumping
Passive ballast transfer (trim) Inter-cell valves between longitudinal ballast cells. In a pitching vessel, opening a valve allows water to flow by gravity with each pitch cycle — net transfer in selected direction over hours. Open/close selected valve — then unattended Trim and list correction without pumping — pitch motion does the work
Roll inertia — corner tanks Header tanks at maximum beam radius (~9.3m from centreline). Mass moment of inertia contribution: 251,000kg × 9.3² × 2 = ~43,400,000 kg·m². Vessel slow to start and stop rolling. None — maintain tanks full during rough weather Longer roll period — comfortable motion for animals, particularly in Phase 1 when seas most violent and tanks fullest
Cedar aromatic insect management Cedar of Lebanon used for internal fitout, promenade lining and cage construction. Aromatic oils (thujaplicins, cedrol) continuously off-gas from ~8,000–10,000 m² of cedar surface area throughout voyage. None Passive fly, mite, flea and parasite repellent. Antifungal and antibacterial in high-humidity animal environment. Counteracts manure odours.
Promenade closed-loop garden Bird cages in atrium above 2m deposit guano by gravity onto planting strip below. Guano fertilises vegetable/sprout beds. Sprouting trays convert bulk dry grain to fresh fodder ×6 volume. Minimal — tend plants on welfare rounds Fresh food supplement for animals and crew. Closed nutrient loop — waste becomes food.
Zone 1 waste self-management Birds and reptiles on mesh cage floors — droppings fall through, desiccate in subfloor litter layer. Low moisture, low volume per animal. Self-managing over 60-day cycles. Bi-monthly subfloor cleanout only Largest animal category (by number) requires minimal waste management
Zone 3 gravity waste drainage Large animals on top deck adjacent to moon pools. Wash-down zone drains by gravity through short sewer run directly to moon pools. Moon pools drain to sea. Periodic wash-down with water High-output animals managed with minimal infrastructure — gravity does the work
Rooster chronometer Clean fowl (7 pairs minimum) have free-running circadian clocks accurate to ±15 minutes over 24 hours, maintained without light cues. Flock synchronisation self-corrects individual drift. Crow audible throughout all three decks. None — impossible to ignore 😄 Accurate daily timekeeping in a sealed vessel with no sky visibility. Enables the precise calendar maintained in Noah's logbook (Genesis 7–8).
Torpor — metabolic self-regulation Small mammals and reptiles in dark, cool, confined conditions reduce metabolic rate 70–80%. Food and water consumption drops proportionally. Not full hibernation — responsive state. None — animals self-regulate Reduces daily food requirement from ~300kg to ~30kg fresh equivalent for small animals. Reduces water consumption from ~15 m³/day to ~6 m³/day overall. Makes 8-person management feasible.
Vegetation mat wave damping (providential) Pre-flood forests ripped up by flood, floating as vast mats. Dense vegetation absorbs 50–75% of wave energy through viscous drag and fetch reduction. Mat persists through Phase 1 and much of Phase 2. None — not a designed system Reduces effective wave height during most violent phase by 50–75%. Allows Phase 1 survival without the full structural loading the solid skin is designed for.
Kopher pitch self-sealing Pine resin pitch impregnated throughout hull skin layers. Pitch softens slightly in warm conditions, flows into micro-gaps, re-seals on cooling. Continuous slow self-healing of minor seam openings. None for minor sealing — onboard pitch cooker for repairs Hull maintains watertightness without constant caulking maintenance. Cross-laminated skin means no continuous seam path — all layers must fail simultaneously for a leak.

For 8 adults, this represents less than 1 hour per person per day on average — confirming that passive systems make the voyage operationally viable for a family crew. The name Noah (nûaḥ — rest) is an apt description of the operational regime. 😄
