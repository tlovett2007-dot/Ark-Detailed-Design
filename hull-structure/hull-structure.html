1. Hull Skin Specification

The hull skin is a structural sandwich — not conventional frame-and-plank construction. The skin itself acts as a longitudinal box girder, resisting hogging and sagging over the 155.4m hull length. This is the only wooden solution viable at this scale, beyond the practical limit of conventional wooden shipbuilding (~90m, Brunel's threshold).

Layered Cross-Section

LayerSpecificationMetricStructural role
Outer planking Triple laminate, 3 × 5" 381mm Outer flange of box girder — compression in hogging. Sacrificial outer layer for abrasion/impact.
Frame / web Close-chocked hardwood frames, ~2ft depth ~610mm Shear web between flanges. Transverse bending resistance (hydrostatic pressure over 22.7m span).
Inner ceiling Double laminate, 2 × 6" 305mm Inner flange of box girder — tension in hogging. Cross-laminated for longitudinal continuity.
Total skin thickness ~1,296mm → 1.6m nominal
Why 1.6m: Raw scantling calculation gives ~1,296mm. Rounded to 1.6m to allow for frame irregularities, pitch sealing layers, and the outer kopher (pitch) waterproofing coat. The 1.6m figure is used in all volume calculations.
Frame depth under review: 2ft (610mm) frames selected as structural optimum — sufficient for transverse hydrostatic bending over 22.7m span, while preserving maximum longitudinal fibre continuity in the laminate flanges. The conflict: deeper frames resist transverse loads better but interrupt longitudinal fibres. Minimum frame depth from bending calc: ~17" (430mm). 2ft provides adequate margin.

2. Frame System — Close-Chocked Construction

Conventional wooden ships have widely-spaced frames with open gaps between them. This Ark design uses close-chocked frames — frames packed so tightly that tapered hardwood wedges fill all remaining gaps, creating a near-solid transverse wall.

Chock Material — High-Density Hardwood Wedges

Cedar fill (as used by Bell) is superseded here by tapered wedges of ultra-dense hardwood (lignum vitae type, or pre-flood equivalent with SG >1.0), driven to full compression between frames from alternating sides.

PropertyCedar fillDense hardwood wedges
Installation methodCut to fit, placed, pinnedTapered, driven to compression — pre-stressed joint
Shrinkage behaviourGap may open on dryingRe-drive wedges as timber seasons — self-tightening

Density~380 kg/m³ (floats)>1,000 kg/m³ (sinks)
MaintenanceRepack if gaps openRe-drive from inside — accessible during voyage
Wood shrinkage: The primary risk with solid-chocked construction is gap opening as timber dries and shrinks. Dense hardwood wedges driven from alternating sides address this — any shrinkage allows re-driving. The Ark interior remains humid from animal respiration throughout the voyage, reducing drying. Pre-flood timber was likely green (unseasoned) at construction; 100 years of construction time allowed seasoning before launch.

3. Bronze Spider Straps

At major timber junctions — particularly where longitudinal skin members meet transverse frames — conventional timber joinery (mortice, tenon, treenail) is insufficient for fully reversing wave loads. Bronze spider straps provide ductile moment connections that can absorb both tension and compression alternately without loosening.

Specification

ElementSpecification
AlloyHigh-tin malleable bronze (~14–18% tin) — maximises cold-working ductility after casting
FormCast spider shape — central hub with 4–6 radiating arms
Hub diameter~150mm
Arm dimensions~50mm wide × 10mm thick × 300mm long
InstallationCast to approximate shape, then hammered cold around the timber junction to conform exactly
FixingSteel nail through pre-drilled holes in each arm, pinned into timber
SealingPitch-impregnated fabric over strap and junction — waterproofing and corrosion protection

Why High-Tin Bronze

Tin content controls the ductility/hardness balance in bronze. At 14–18% tin, the alloy remains castable (good fluidity, sharp detail) but can be cold-worked significantly — hammered flat against the irregular surface of a round or hewn timber junction without cracking. The ductility allows micro-yielding under the fully reversing wave loads that would fracture a harder alloy.

Pre-flood metallurgical precedent: Genesis 4:22 — Tubal-Cain, "forger of all instruments of bronze and iron," lived just a few generations before Noah. High-tin bronze metallurgy was current, available, and sophisticated at the time of construction.

Future Exhibition Proposal

A full-scale structural demonstration section — genuine hardwood frames, dense hardwood wedge chocking, real cast and hammer-formed bronze spider straps, pitch-impregnated fabric sealing — would be the first physical realisation of this hull technology. Not a replica or model — the actual specified construction method, built to full scale, touchable and smellable. Proposed for Ark Encounter or similar institution.

Cedar aromatic system: Cedar of Lebanon used for internal fitout, promenade lining, cage construction and ceiling panels provides a passive insect/parasite management system. Cedar oil (thujaplicins, cedrol) is a proven biocide, insect repellent and antifungal agent. In an enclosed vessel with thousands of animals over 371 days, insect and parasite load is a genuine animal welfare problem. The cedar interior passively addresses this at no operational cost — and incidentally counteracts manure odours. Surface area ~8,000–10,000 m² of aromatic cedar continuously off-gassing throughout the voyage.

Gopher Wood

Genesis 6:14 specifies gopher wood (Hebrew: עֲצֵי-גֹפֶר). The term is a hapax legomenon — appearing exactly once in the entire Bible. No subsequent reference allows identification. Candidates include cypress (gofer → kuparissos), a pre-flood timber kind now extinct or diversified beyond recognition, or possibly a laminated construction process rather than a species name.

The pre-flood single landmass (Pangaea equivalent) placed all timber kinds in proximity. Post-flood continental dispersal sent the descendants of pre-flood timber baramins to geographically separated locations (Douglas fir to Pacific Northwest, teak to Southeast Asia, cedar to Lebanon and Pacific Northwest, kauri to New Zealand). The ancestral gopher wood kind may have been the generalised progenitor of several modern timber families — diversified post-flood beyond recognition, hence the word becoming obsolete.

5. Waterproofing — Kopher System

Genesis 6:14 specifies coating the Ark with kopher (כֹּפֶר) — pitch/bitumen — inside and out. The Hebrew root kaphar means to cover, atone, make reconciliation.

Onboard Pitch Cooker

A small bronze pot over a controlled fire, located in the stern service compartment. Pre-cut fabric strips stored dry alongside. Pitched fabric functions as the universal repair material throughout the voyage — superior to any modern equivalent in wet conditions:

6. Longitudinal Structural Challenge — Hogging and Sagging

At 155.4m, the Ark exceeds Brunel's identified practical limit for wooden construction (~91m / 300ft), beyond which hogging and sagging loads cause progressive structural failure in conventional frame-and-plank ships. The solid laminated skin box girder is the only wooden solution viable at this length.

Transverse hydrostatic pressure at bottom: P = ρgh = 1,025 × 9.81 × 15.54m = ~156 kPa Frame bending moment (simply supported, 22.7m span): M = wL²/8 = 156 × 22.7² / 8 = ~9,996 kNm per metre of length Required section modulus at 10 MPa allowable timber stress: Z = M/σ = 9,996,000 / 10,000 = ~1,000,000 cm³/m Back-calculated frame depth at 300mm frame width: bd²/6 = 1,000,000 → d = ~447mm ≈ 18" minimum Selected frame depth: 2ft (610mm) — provides adequate margin above 18" minimum while limiting interruption of longitudinal skin fibres.
Longitudinal bending moment calculation pending: The hogging/sagging bending moment for a 155.4m hull in the design wave condition has not yet been calculated. This will determine whether the laminate skin thickness (381mm outer + 305mm inner) provides adequate longitudinal section modulus. CAD geometry required for full calculation.
