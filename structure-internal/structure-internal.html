Internal structure comprises all structural elements within the hull skin envelope — deck systems, longitudinal beams, vertical posts, promenade structure, and the bronze spider strap junction system. Internal structure must transfer all deck and cargo loads to the hull skin box girder, while providing the spatial framework for three decks of animal accommodation.

The Asymmetry — tachtiy vs ri'shon

The Hebrew word for "first" is ri'shon (רִאשׁוֹן) — used constantly throughout Genesis. God could have written ri'shon, sheni, shelishi (first, second, third). He did not. Instead tachtiy (lower/beneath — positional) replaces ri'shon (first — ordinal) for the bottom level. This is deliberate. Tachtiy describes something's spatial relationship to a reference point above it, not its position in a sequence. You do not say "lower" unless there is an implied reference point above.

The sequence is not first → second → third (counting up from the bottom) but lower → two → three — measured from a middle reference point. The most natural reference point: the entry door.

Four functional levels from three Hebrew words — the entry level implied but not numbered, consistent with the door being the natural reference point from which everything else is described.

Engineering Support for Middle-Deck Door

Door positionAssessment
Bottom deck (Lowers)Too close to waterline — wave ingress risk during loading ✗
Middle deck (Twos)Above wave risk, manageable gangway angle, practical for large animal loading ✓
Top deck (Threes)Too high — impractical gangway geometry for large animals ✗

Modern car ferries and ro-ro vessels load at mid-hull level for exactly these reasons. At 15.72m hull height with middle-deck door at ~7.86m above keel — above waterline at target draft, practical gangway angle. Text and engineering converge independently.

Deck numbering convention: maritime bottom-up — Deck 1 (Lowers), Deck 2 (Twos), Deck 3 (Threes). Entry door at Deck 2 level.

Longitudinal Deck Beams (Stringers)

Running longitudinally under each deck, parallel to the keel, stringers distribute deck loads to transverse frames and provide longitudinal stability to the deck system. They also contribute to the box girder longitudinal section modulus — a secondary structural benefit.

5. Bronze Spider Straps

At every major timber junction — deck beam to hull frame, post to deck beam, portal frame corners, bulkhead to hull connections — conventional timber joinery (mortice, tenon, treenail) is insufficient for fully reversing wave loads over 371 days. Bronze spider straps provide ductile moment connections.

Why Reversing Loads Defeat Timber Joinery

In a seaway, wave loads alternate between hogging and sagging — every structural junction sees tension then compression then tension again, cycling at the wave period (~14–22 seconds). Timber joints that rely on compression (mortice and tenon, bolted connections) work well under static or one-direction loads but gradually loosen under reversing loads as timber fibres crush and compact. After thousands of cycles the joint becomes loose and eventually fails.

Bronze spider straps resist both tension and compression — they cannot loosen under reversing loads. The ductility allows micro-yielding rather than fracture.

Metallurgical Precedent

Genesis 4:22 — Tubal-Cain, "forger of all instruments of bronze and iron" — lived four generations before Noah. High-tin bronze metallurgy was current, sophisticated and available at the time of Ark construction.
