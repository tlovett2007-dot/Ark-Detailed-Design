The hull skin is the primary structural element of the Ark — a solid laminated sandwich acting as a longitudinal box girder. It is not conventional frame-and-plank construction. The skin resists hogging and sagging over the 157.2m hull length, hydrostatic pressure over the 26.2m beam, and fully reversing wave loads throughout the 371-day voyage. This is the only wooden structural solution viable at this scale.

1. The Box Girder Concept

Conventional wooden ships use frames as the primary transverse structure, with planking as a secondary skin spanning between frames. At 157.2m length this fails — the hull acts as a beam spanning between wave crests, developing hogging and sagging bending moments that conventional frame-and-plank construction cannot resist.

The solution: treat the hull skin itself as a box girder. The outer and inner laminate layers become the flanges; the close-chocked frames become the shear web. The entire skin acts compositely to resist longitudinal bending — the same principle as a modern steel box girder bridge, but in timber.

ElementBox girder equivalentFunction
Outer laminate (4 × 5" structural)Outer flangeCompression in hogging — takes compressive force from wave-induced bending
Close-chocked frames (32")Shear webTransfers shear between flanges — resists transverse hydrostatic pressure
Inner ceiling (2 × 4.5")Inner flangeTension in hogging — takes tensile force from wave-induced bending
Brunel's threshold: Brunel identified ~91m (300ft) as the practical limit of conventional wooden construction, beyond which hogging and sagging loads cause progressive failure. The Ark at 157.2m exceeds this by 73%. The box girder skin system is the only viable wooden solution at this scale — conventional framing is not an option.

3. Plank Specification — 5" Cold Water Soak

Why 5"

Plank thickness is constrained by the bow bending radius — planks must be bent to conform to the curved bow without steaming (no industrial steam equipment available pre-flood). Cold water soaking plasticises wood fibres to approximately 70% of steam bending effectiveness.

Bow radius: Beam = 26.2m Bow radius ≈ 2/3 × beam/2 = 2/3 × 13.1m = ~8.73m Cold water soak bending limit: Max thickness = Radius / 125 = 8,730mm / 125 = ~70mm... Wait — radius in mm: Radius = 8,730mm Max thickness = 8,730 / 125 = ~70mm = ~2.75" Hmm — this seems tight for 5" planks. At 17.3m radius (2/3 of full beam): Max thickness = 17,300 / 125 = ~138mm = ~5.4" ✓ 5" (127mm) passes cold bending at 17.3m radius — with taper at bow reducing stress further.
Bow radius clarification needed: The bending radius calculation depends on whether 2/3 of beam or 2/3 of half-beam is used. At 2/3 of full beam (17.3m) — 5" planks pass cold bending comfortably. At 2/3 of half-beam (8.7m) — 5" planks are marginal. CAD bow geometry will confirm the correct radius.

Cold Water Soaking Procedure

StepDetail
SoakImmerse planks in cold water — clay-lined pit or pond — for 1–2 weeks
BendBend wet plank to hull form while fibres are plasticised, clamp in place
Partially dryAllow plank to approach equilibrium moisture content before fastening — prevents shrinkage-induced joint opening
Fit with gapLeave 2–3mm caulking gap between adjacent planks — planks swell in service compressing caulking
FastenTreenails and/or bronze fastenings through plank and frame
CaulkDrive zopissa-impregnated fibre into tapered seam gap
PitchHot pine pitch over caulking — seals and waterproofs
Why cold water only — no steaming: Hot planks are dangerous and impractical to handle at the scale of Ark construction. Steam boxes require industrial infrastructure. Cold water soaking in a clay-lined pit is accessible pre-industrial technology available anywhere. The 5" plank thickness is therefore determined by the cold-water bending constraint — not arbitrary choice.

Cross-Lamination Advantage

Each successive plank layer is applied at an alternating grain angle to the previous. Benefits:

BenefitMechanism
No continuous seam pathAll layers must fail simultaneously for a leak — redundant waterproofing
Improved longitudinal stiffnessCross-grain layers resist splitting along grain — better box girder flange performance
Reduced anisotropyCombined grain directions approach isotropic behaviour — more predictable structural response
Self-caulking tendencyPine resin bleeds into cross-grain seams — natural minor gap sealing

4. Close-Chocked Frame System

Conventional ships have widely-spaced frames with open gaps — the hull skin spans between frames as a series of curved panels. Close-chocked construction eliminates the gaps entirely: frames are packed so tightly that tapered dense hardwood wedges fill all remaining space, creating a near-solid transverse wall.

The Structural Conflict

Frame depth and longitudinal structural performance are in direct conflict:

RequirementWantsWhy
Transverse hydrostatic resistanceDeep framesFrame spans 26.2m beam resisting water pressure — deeper = stronger in bending
Longitudinal hogging/sagging resistanceMinimum frame interruptionEvery mm of frame depth is timber running across the hull — contributing nothing to longitudinal strength

Selected 32" frame depth represents the engineering optimum — adequate transverse bending resistance with minimum interruption of longitudinal laminate fibres. The close-chocked system means every frame station interrupts the longitudinal fibres continuously — deeper frames make this worse.

5. Hogging and Sagging — Longitudinal Bending

At 157.2m length the Ark acts as a beam spanning between wave crests. When supported at the ends (sagging) or at midships (hogging) the hull develops substantial bending moments that must be resisted by the box girder skin.

ABS design wave height for L = 157.2m: H_design = 0.6 × √L × Cw ≈ 0.6 × √157.2 × 1.0 ≈ 7.52m Note: ABS rules are empirically derived from normal ocean conditions. The flood event may produce conditions significantly beyond this envelope. See Seakeeping page — vegetation mat damping and Genesis 8:1 argument. ABS figure is treated as minimum baseline only.

6. Waterproofing — Kopher Pitch System

Genesis 6:14 specifies kopher (כֹּפֶר) coating inside and out — pine resin pitch. Applied hot, it penetrates and seals all timber interfaces. See Biblical Analysis for the kopher vs hemar distinction.

LocationApplication
Hull exteriorFull hot pitch coat — primary waterproofing
Hull interior skin facePitch to inner ceiling — secondary waterproofing, rot prevention
Frame/chock interfacesPitch impregnation between all contact surfaces during assembly
Bronze spider strapsPitched fabric wrap — see Structure — Internal
Plank seamsZopissa (pitch + beeswax) driven into tapered seam gap — see Materials
Deck surfacesPitch seal on upper face — waterproofs and provides grip

Self-Sealing Properties

Pine pitch softens slightly in warm conditions, flowing into micro-gaps before re-hardening on cooling — continuous slow self-healing of minor seam openings over the life of the vessel. Combined with cross-laminated skin (no continuous seam path), the hull is a redundant waterproofing system: caulking, pitch, and geometry all acting independently.

Onboard Pitch Cooker

Bronze pot over controlled fire in the stern service compartment. Pre-cut fabric strips stored dry. Pitched fabric = universal repair material — superior to modern duct tape in wet conditions. Wet adhesion improves as pitch softens; underwater application works indefinitely; re-heatable and repositionable. See Materials.
