Water Systems

The Ark's water system is entirely passive — no pumps, no crew operation under normal conditions. It operates as a two-tier gravity system: bulk ballast/storage below, header tanks above, with rainwater harvesting and a novel tilt-weir elevation device providing replenishment.

2. Bulk Ballast / Fresh Water Zone

The ballast zone sits below the Deck 1 floor, within the curved bilge section of the hull. It serves the dual purpose of stability ballast (lowering the centre of gravity) and strategic fresh water reserve.

Geometry

Ballast zone depth: ~2.0m Flat bottom width: ~20m (inside bilge curves) Usable length: ~125.4m (excluding bow/stern compartments) Maximum volume: 2.0 × 20 × 125.4 = ~5,016 m³ Plus bilge curve contribution: ~15% = ~5,770 m³ total capacity Working figure: ~4,750 m³ (allowing for baffle structure volume)

Baffle System

An undivided tank of 4,750 m³ would have catastrophic free surface effect, destabilising the vessel in any seaway. The ballast zone is divided by:

Baffle typeNumberSpacingFunction
Transverse bulkheads 6 (shared with main hull bulkheads) ~18m Primary longitudinal subdivision — 7 bays
Longitudinal baffles 2 (centreline + one intermediate each side) ~6.7m Transverse subdivision — 3 cells per bay = 21 cells total
Free surface moment per cell (after full baffling): I_fs = ρ × l × b³/12 = 1,025 × 18 × 6.7³/12 = ~461,000 kg·m² (reduced from ~12,300,000 kg·m² unbaffled — factor of 27 improvement)

Passive Trim Control

Inter-cell valves between longitudinal cells allow passive ballast transfer for trim and list correction. In a pitching vessel, water flows through open valves by gravity — pitch motion does the pumping. Transfer direction is controlled by which valve is opened; rate is controlled by valve aperture.

Operational procedure: Noah opens the appropriate inter-cell valve, returns to bed. Pitch motion transfers water over hours. Closes valve when desired trim is achieved. No crew effort required beyond valve operation.

Fresh Water Requirement vs Capacity

Voyage duration: 371 days Rain ceases (Genesis): Day ~150 Dry phase duration: ~220 days Animal daily consumption: 6,000 animals × 1.0 L/day (torpor-reduced) = 6,000 L/day = 6 m³/day Human daily consumption: 8 people × 3 L/day = ~0.024 m³/day (negligible) Dry phase requirement: 220 days × 6 m³/day = 1,320 m³ Safety margin (20%): +264 m³ Total bulk water needed: ~1,584 m³ Ballast zone capacity: ~4,750 m³ Surplus available for stability ballast: ~3,166 m³ ✓
Comparison with Woodmorappe: Woodmorappe allocated ~10% of hull volume for water (~4,500 m³ equivalent on this hull), apparently carrying a full year's supply. This design requires only ~1,584 m³ for the dry phase because: (1) fewer animals (~6,000 vs 16,000), (2) torpor-reduced consumption (1 L/day vs ~2.5 L/day normal), (3) rainwater harvesting covers the wet phase (days 1–150), (4) the ballast zone serves dual function. Woodmorappe did not account for rainwater harvesting despite the vessel having a 3,875 m² roof catchment area.

Voyage Phases

Roof Catchment Calculation

Roof catchment area: 155.4m × 25.9m = ~4,025 m² (Slightly larger due to roof slope — actual catchment ~4,200 m²) At 5mm/day light rain: 4,200 × 0.005 = 21.0 m³/day At 10mm/day: 4,200 × 0.010 = 42.0 m³/day At 20mm event: 4,200 × 0.020 = 84.0 m³/day = ~14 days animal supply in one shower Daily animal requirement: ~6 m³/day Even 1.5mm/day rain covers full daily consumption ✓

Collection System

Rainwater runs off the roof slope toward the outer edges, where a low timber parapet runs the full length of the weatherdeck perimeter. The parapet serves dual functions:

FunctionDescription
Wave deflector Deflects green water (waves washing over deck) away from the ṣohar opening — first line of defence against interior flooding
Rainwater gutter Retains rainwater in the gutter between parapet and ṣohar coaming, channelling it to corner downpipes into header tanks

Self-Regulating Flap Valves

Gaps in the parapet fitted with simple flap valves (timber/bronze, hinged at top):

ConditionValve stateResult
Calm / light rain Open (gravity) Rainwater drains inboard through gaps → header tanks fill
Green water on deck Closed (wave pressure) No saltwater contamination of fresh header tanks
Self-regulating logic: Green water events (rough weather) close the valves automatically by wave pressure — exactly when contamination risk is highest. In calm conditions when collection is safe, valves open by gravity. No crew operation required. The system also self-times — rough weather that prevents collection coincides with full tanks from prior rain; calm periods allow collection when seas are benign.

4. Header Tanks

Location and Rationale

Header tanks are positioned in the outboard roof corners — the triangular dead space where the roof slope meets the hull top. This location optimises five criteria simultaneously:

CriterionCorner tanksCentral roof tanks
Mass moment of inertia (roll comfort)Maximum r² from centreline ✓Reduced r²
Usable space consumedZero — dead triangular space ✓Competes with atrium
ṣohar light/air blockedNo — tanks are outboard ✓Potentially yes
Rainwater collection proximityAt collection point — roof drains to corners ✓Requires additional pipes
Gravity feed to all decksHighest point in vessel ✓Same ✓
Structural integrationInto hull/roof junction — shared walls ✓Freestanding structure

Volume and Specification

Tank geometry (triangular corner cross-section): Base at hull inner face: ~1.0m Height at deepest point: ~1.0m Cross-sectional area: ~0.5 m² (triangular) Both sides, full usable length 125.4m: Volume = 0.5 × 125.4 × 2 = ~125 m²... Revised (from CAD, 1m wide service column): 1.0m wide × 1.0m deep × 125.4m × 2 sides = ~251 m³

Adequacy Check

Animal daily consumption: 6,000 × 1.0 L = 6,000 L/day = 6 m³/day Header tank volume: 251 m³ Days supply without refill: 251 ÷ 6 = ~42 days Cage reservoir additional buffer: sized for 2–4 weeks Total buffer before bulk tanks needed: ~6–10 weeks ✓ This comfortably spans the period between rain events during the wet phase, and provides time to operate the tilt-weir pump during calm dry-phase periods.

Stability Contribution

Header tank mass (full): 251 m³ × 1,000 kg/m³ = 251 tonnes Radius from centreline: ~9.3m (outboard corner position) Mass moment of inertia contribution: I = 251,000 kg × (9.3m)² × 2 sides = ~43,400,000 kg·m² This is a meaningful contribution to roll inertia → longer roll period → more comfortable motion, particularly during the wet phase (days 1–150) when tanks are full and seas are most severe.

6. Tilt-Weir Pump

A novel passive water elevation device using vessel pitch/roll motion to transfer water upward through a series of overflow cells — no valves, no moving parts, no crew operation. Used to replenish header tanks from bulk ballast zone during the dry phase of the voyage.

Novel device: Wave-powered pumps using overflow principles exist (Jobb Wave Pump, 2009 patent) but use fixed coastal installations with oscillating air column drive. The tilt-weir pump uses vessel motion itself as the drive, with internal sequential overflow cells. No prior art identified for this specific application. The device was developed in the context of this Ark research.

Operating Principle

A series of open troughs arranged in a zigzag, each slightly higher than the previous, connected by overflow walls of incrementally increasing height. Vessel tilt causes water to spill over the lower end of each overflow wall into the next trough. The wall height prevents return flow. Net result: water ratchets upward one step per tilt cycle.

No valves required — just geometry: Each overflow wall slightly higher than the previous Vessel tilts → water surface tilts → water spills over lower wall edge Vessel returns upright → wall too high for backflow Next tilt → water spills further forward Net: water moves uphill one cell per tilt cycle

Transfer Rate Estimate

Pitch cycle period: ~8 seconds (typical swell) Cycles per hour: 450 Efficient cycles (10% at moderate swell): 45 per hour Volume per successful overflow: 2.0m × 0.3m deep × spillage fraction ~0.05m = ~5 litres Transfer rate: 45 × 5L = ~225 litres/hour = ~5.4 m³/day Daily animal consumption: ~6 m³/day Tilt-weir pump nearly matches daily consumption in moderate swell ✓ In heavier weather (sea state 6-7): significantly higher transfer rate Header tanks (251 m³) can be replenished from empty in ~47 days of moderate pumping
Transfer rate to be confirmed: The above estimate uses simplified assumptions. Wave frequency, trough resonance, and actual tilt angles need modelling. The pump is supplementary to rainwater harvesting — not the primary supply. Even a fraction of the estimated rate is adequate given the large header tank buffer (42 days) and cage reservoir buffer (2–4 weeks).

7. Animal Water Consumption

Torpor Reduction

Modern live animal transport ships operate with fully conscious, active animals — and even these show 20–30% reduced feed and water intake compared to animals at pasture (industry standard observation). The Ark's conditions would drive further reduction:

FactorEffect on water consumption
Darkness / low lightReduced activity → reduced metabolic rate
Cool temperaturesLower metabolic rate → less water needed
Torpor / semi-hibernation (small mammals, reptiles)70–80% reduction in consumption
No growth, no reproduction (maintenance only)Minimum metabolic water requirement
Stress-induced anorexia (initial period)Further short-term reduction
Population breakdown: Small mammals, reptiles, most birds (~70%): 70-80% reduction → ~0.2 L/day avg Medium animals (~25%): 30% reduction → ~2.0 L/day avg Large animals (~5%): 20% reduction → ~15 L/day avg Weighted average: (0.7 × 0.2) + (0.25 × 2.0) + (0.05 × 15) = 0.14 + 0.50 + 0.75 = ~1.4 L/day/animal At 6,000 animals: 6,000 × 1.4 = 8,400 L/day Conservative working figure used: 6,000 L/day (1.0 L/day avg — more conservative)
Comparison with Woodmorappe: Woodmorappe did not explicitly invoke torpor-reduced consumption, did not account for rainwater harvesting, and allocated ~10% of hull volume for water. On an equivalent hull this would be ~6,250 m³. This design requires only ~1,584 m³ for the critical dry phase — a factor of ~4 reduction — explained by fewer animals, torpor reduction, and the dual-purpose ballast/storage system.
