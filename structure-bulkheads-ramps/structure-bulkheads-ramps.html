Structure — Bulkheads & Ramps

Cubit: 0.524m (Royal Egyptian)  |  Last revised: 2026-07-23 12:22 AEST  |  Status: Working document
Home › Structure — Bulkheads & Ramps

Transverse bulkheads divide the hull into longitudinal compartments — providing structural rigidity, limiting flood damage propagation, separating functional zones, and carrying transverse loads from deck and hull to the keel. Internal ramps provide animal and crew access between the three deck levels without compromising the structural integrity of the bulkhead system.

1. Transverse Bulkhead System

Number and Spacing

Six transverse bulkheads are specified in the volume tally, dividing the main hull into seven longitudinal bays. This is the minimum number consistent with structural and functional requirements.

Structural Functions

FunctionMechanism
Transverse rigidityBulkheads act as deep transverse frames — resist distortion of the rectangular cross-section under hydrostatic pressure and wave loads
Longitudinal load transferBulkheads collect deck beam loads from both sides and transfer them to the keel via posts — reducing point loads on keel
Flood damage limitationIf the hull skin is breached in one bay, flooding is contained — vessel can survive limited flooding without sinking
Racking resistanceBulkheads resist transverse racking of the hull under roll loads — prevent the rectangular cross-section from parallelogramming
Zone separationSeparate animal species zones, waste management zones, ballast cells — operational as well as structural function

Volume Deduction

Six bulkheads × full internal cross-section: Internal beam: ~23.1m Internal height (animal zone): ~11.76m Bulkhead thickness: ~400mm (assumed) Volume per bulkhead: 23.1 × 11.76 × 0.4 = ~109 m³ Six bulkheads: ~654 m³ Plus bulkhead framing timbers: ~886 m³ total — close to tally figure (Tally shows ~1,540 m³ for six transverse bulkheads — includes associated structure) Pending CAD confirmation.

2. Ballast Zone Longitudinal Baffles

The ballast zone below Deck 1 is divided into 21 longitudinal cells by baffles — not full bulkheads but partial-height dividers allowing controlled water transfer between cells.

Baffle Function

FunctionMechanism
Free surface effect reduction21 cells vs 1 large tank — dramatically reduces the destabilising effect of free water surface sloshing
Trim controlOpening/closing inter-cell valves transfers water longitudinally — adjusts trim without pumping
List correctionTransferring water port/starboard corrects any list from asymmetric loading
Passive trim by pitchOpening a valve in a pitching vessel allows net water transfer in the selected direction over hours — see Passive Systems

Free Surface Effect — Why 21 Cells

Free surface effect on GM: Single ballast tank (full beam × full length): I_free_surface = (1/12) × L × B³ = (1/12) × 125.4 × 23.1³ = ~1,625,000 m⁴ GG' reduction = I / V = 1,625,000 / 20,317 = ~80m — catastrophic! 😄 21 cells (each ~6m wide × ~6m long): I per cell = (1/12) × 6 × 6³ = 108 m⁴ 21 cells total: 21 × 108 = ~2,268 m⁴ GG' reduction = 2,268 / 20,317 = ~0.11m — manageable ✓ Conclusion: cell subdivision is not optional — a single large ballast tank would destroy all stability. 21 cells reduces free surface effect by ~99%.
The 21-cell number: Derived from the requirement to keep free surface GG' correction below ~0.1m — maintaining adequate GM after ballast free surface deduction. Fewer larger cells would compromise stability; more smaller cells adds unnecessary construction complexity.

3. Internal Ramp System

Purpose

Three deck levels must be accessible to:

UserRequirement
Animals (loading/unloading)Gentle gradient — large animals cannot navigate steep stairs. Ramp must handle cattle, horses, giraffes, camels.
Crew (daily operations)Rapid access between decks for welfare rounds, waste management, emergency response
Fodder and waste (barrows)Loaded barrows must be wheeled between decks — flat gradient essential
Water systemsTilt-weir pump troughs run between decks — ramp structure integrates with water system

Ramp Specification

ParameterSpecificationBasis
Gradient~1:6 maximum (9.5°)Maximum for loaded barrow — steeper impractical for heavy waste loads
Width~3m minimumTwo-way animal traffic + barrow passing width
SurfaceRough-sawn timber with transverse cleatsAnti-slip for animal hooves, pitched for waterproofing
Side barriersSolid timber walls to 1.5mAnimal containment — prevent falls off ramp edge
Total height to cover~3.92m deck-to-deckClear headroom per deck at REC 524mm cubit

Ramp Geometry

Ramp to cover 3.92m height at 1:6 gradient: Horizontal run = 3.92 × 6 = ~23.5m per deck transition For three decks (Deck 1 → 2 → 3): Total ramp length: 2 × 23.5m = ~47m of ramp run At 3m width × 47m length: Ramp floor area: ~141 m² Volume deduction (ramp structure): ~2,348 m³ (includes ramp void space, structure, barriers) — consistent with volume tally figure ✓

Ramp Location

Ramps positioned to serve both animal loading and operational access simultaneously:

RequirementImplication for location
Animal loading at entry door (Deck 2)Ramp from Deck 2 → Deck 1 and Deck 2 → Deck 3 adjacent to entry door zone
Waste barrow route to moon poolRamp must provide clear barrow path from Zone 2 (Deck 2) to moon pool zone (Deck 3 area)
Structural — avoid bulkhead zonesRamps positioned within bays, not cutting through bulkheads

Ramp location TBD from CAD. The competing requirements suggest ramps near the entry door zone (midship area) for animal loading convenience, with a secondary ramp near the moon pool zone for waste management. Two ramp systems — one forward of centre, one aft — may be required.

4. Ring Frames at Openings

Every penetration through a structural member — bulkhead doors, deck hatches, moon pool trunk, ṣohar opening in roof — creates a stress concentration requiring ring frame reinforcement.

Entry Door — Critical Opening

Genesis 6:16 specifies a door (Hebrew: petach — opening, entrance) in the side of the Ark. This is the largest single penetration in the hull skin — a structural discontinuity in the primary box girder at the most critical location (side of hull, subject to wave pressure and green water).

Structural challengeSolution
Interrupts longitudinal laminate continuity on one sideExtra laminate layers around door perimeter — restore section modulus locally
Wave pressure on door faceMassive timber door — solid laminated construction, sealed with pitched fabric
Door sealing for 371 daysGenesis 7:16 — "the LORD shut him in." Divine sealing — engineering implication: watertight beyond normal timber door capability. Pitch-impregnated fabric surround + heavy timber batten sealing.
Location stress concentrationBronze spider straps at all four corners of door opening — ductile moment connections
Genesis 7:16 — "the LORD shut him in": The divine sealing of the door is noted as an engineering specification point — the door achieved watertightness beyond what Noah's construction alone might guarantee. This is consistent with the design philosophy throughout: passive systems handle the analysable envelope; divine provision handles the remainder. A pitched and battered timber door is engineeringly sound; divine sealing provides the margin. See Seakeeping — Genesis 8:1 for the same argument applied to seakeeping.
