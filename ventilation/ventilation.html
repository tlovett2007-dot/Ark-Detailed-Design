Ventilation

Cubit: 0.524m (Royal Egyptian)  |  Last revised: July 2026  |  Status: Working document
Home › Ventilation

Ventilation is the primary life support system for 6,000 animals and 8 humans over 371 days in a sealed timber vessel. It must manage oxygen supply, CO₂ removal, humidity, temperature, and air quality — all passively, without mechanical systems, using only hull geometry and natural convection.

1. The Ventilation Challenge

The ventilation system must be entirely passive — no fans, no ducting, no mechanical systems. Eight people cannot operate a mechanical ventilation system for 371 days while also managing 6,000 animals. The vessel geometry must do the work.

2. The ṣohar — Primary Ventilation Feature

Genesis 6:16 specifies the ṣohar (צֹהַר) — a roof opening running the full length of the vessel, 1 cubit (0.524m) high, positioned at the top of the hull below the roof peak. The ṣohar is the defining ventilation feature of the entire design.

The 1-Cubit Coaming

The 0.524m coaming height sits ABOVE the Genesis 30-cubit hull height — it is not included in the main hull dimension. The coaming serves multiple functions:

FunctionMechanism
Wave exclusion0.524m lip prevents green water entering from weatherdeck
Rain damPrevents direct rainfall entry — rain falls on weatherdeck and drains to corner header tanks
Venturi effectWind passing over the coaming creates low pressure above the opening — draws air upward from below
Light admissionDiffuse natural light enters through the full length opening — illuminates the promenade atrium

3. Passive Ventilation Mechanism

Stack Effect (Thermal Convection)

The primary ventilation driver is the stack effect — warm air rises, creating continuous natural convection through the vessel:

Convection path: Animals generate heat → warm air rises ↓ Warm air rises through promenade atrium (full height, 15.72m) ↓ Exits through ṣohar at roof level (highest point) ↓ Fresh cooler air drawn in through outboard vent shafts (lower level) ↓ Air distributed across animal zones ↓ Picks up heat and gases → rises again → cycle continues

Animal Heat as Ventilation Driver

The problem (animal body heat) becomes the solution (convection driver). 6,000 metabolising animals generate substantial heat — estimated:

Heat generation estimate: Small animals (~4,200 at torpor): ~2W each = 8,400W Medium animals (~1,500 at reduced metabolic rate): ~20W each = 30,000W Large animals (~300 at reduced metabolic rate): ~100W each = 30,000W 8 humans: ~80W each = 640W Total: ~69,040W ≈ 69kW continuous heat input This is equivalent to ~690 light bulbs heating the interior continuously. Stack effect convection driven by this heat input is substantial.

Wind-Driven Ventilation

When the vessel is weathervaning (bow into wind), wind passes longitudinally over the ṣohar creating a Venturi low-pressure zone above the opening. This supplements the stack effect — particularly effective in the windy receding phase (days 150–371) when God's drying wind provides a reliable air stream.

Cross Ventilation — ṣohar to Outboard Vents

The outboard service columns (1m wide, both sides, full height) function as vertical vent shafts — fresh air inlets at lower levels balancing the ṣohar exhaust at roof level. The pressure differential between the warm ṣohar exhaust and the cooler outboard inlets drives continuous cross-ventilation.

4. Air Quality Management

Ammonia and Methane

Animal waste produces ammonia (NH₃) from urea decomposition and methane (CH₄) from anaerobic fermentation. Both are:

GasDensity vs airBehaviourImplication
Ammonia NH₃Lighter than air (MW 17 vs 29)Rises naturallyDrawn up through atrium to ṣohar — self-venting ✓
Methane CH₄Lighter than air (MW 16)Rises naturallySame — self-venting through stack effect ✓
CO₂Heavier than air (MW 44)Sinks to floor levelRisk of accumulation at floor level — mitigated by air movement ✓

The fortunate coincidence: the most dangerous gases (ammonia, methane) are lighter than air and rise naturally toward the ṣohar exhaust. The stack effect specifically targets these gases for removal. CO₂, which sinks, is managed by the general air circulation keeping floor-level air moving.

Cedar Aromatic System

Cedar of Lebanon interior fitout provides passive air quality management through continuous off-gassing of aromatic compounds:

CompoundEffectBenefit
ThujaplicinsNatural biocide — antifungal, antibacterialSuppresses mould growth in humid interior
CedrolInsect repellentPassive fly, mite, flea management
TerpenesAromatic, antifungalCounteracts ammonia and manure odours

Surface area: ~8,000–10,000 m² of cedar continuously off-gassing throughout the voyage. The ventilation system draws this cedar-scented air through the animal zones — passive aromatherapy for 6,000 animals. 😄

Header Tank Condensation Effect

The corner header tanks (251 m³ of water at roof level) are cooler than the rising warm animal air. Warm humid air rising through the atrium contacts the cool tank bases — moisture condenses on the tank surfaces, partially dehumidifying the air before it exits through the ṣohar. Passive dehumidification at the point of air exhaust. Condensate drains back down the service column to the ballast zone.

5. Deck-by-Deck Ventilation

DeckVentilation qualityNotes
Deck 3 (Threes — top) Best — directly below ṣohar Large animals and primates assigned here — highest air quality requirements. Warm stale air exits immediately above. Fresh air from outboard vents at this level.
Deck 2 (Twos — middle) Good — mid-height in stack Small and medium mammals. Air quality adequate for torpor-reduced metabolism.
Deck 1 (Lowers — bottom) Least — lowest in stack Reptiles, snakes — lowest metabolic rate, lowest oxygen requirement. CO₂ accumulation risk mitigated by full-length longitudinal vent shaft at this level.
Promenade atrium (all decks) Excellent — direct ṣohar connection Bird cages in atrium — birds most sensitive to air quality. Direct stack effect path to ṣohar exhaust. Best location for sensitive species.
Deck assignment and ventilation: The deck assignments (large animals top, reptiles bottom) are consistent with ventilation requirements as well as waste drainage requirements — both criteria point to the same solution independently. See Animal Zones.

6. Ventilation During Storm Phases

PhaseDaysVentilation conditionNotes
Early flood 1–40 ṣohar partially restricted — green water risk Weatherdeck parapet and coaming provide protection. Stack effect still operates. Reduced natural light.
Mid flood 40–150 Good — storm reducing, ṣohar fully operational Stack effect + wind-driven Venturi. Best ventilation phase.
Receding 150–371 Excellent — God's drying wind provides strong Venturi Sustained directional wind over ṣohar — maximum ventilation performance. Drying wind also reduces interior humidity.
Storm phase ṣohar protection: During severe weather (Phase 1), the combination of weatherdeck parapet (first defence) and 0.524m coaming (second defence) protects the ṣohar from direct wave ingress. However the ventilation rate may be reduced during the most severe conditions. The enclosed timber vessel with 6,000 animals in torpor can tolerate reduced ventilation for limited periods — metabolic oxygen demand is dramatically reduced under torpor conditions.
