Draft & Trim

Last revised: July 2026 | Status: Working document

Home › Draft & Trim

Draft and trim are determined by mass distribution. They cannot be set independently — draft follows from total displacement, trim follows from the longitudinal position of the centre of gravity relative to the centre of buoyancy. Both are tuned through the ballast distribution, primarily rock ballast placed low and aft.

1. The Genesis 7:20 Constraint

"The waters rose and covered the mountains to a depth of fifteen cubits." — Genesis 7:20

This is the primary draft constraint. The interpretation adopted here:

InterpretationAssessment
Water depth above mountain peaks = 15 cubitsRequires Noah to measure depth above submerged peaks — no mechanism available. Rejected.
Noah reporting his known draft = 15 cubitsThe one measurement Noah could make — sounding from the stern. Seamanlike. Adopted.

15 cubits = 15 × 0.524m = 7.86m — measured from the bottom of the stern keel to the waterline at the stern.

This gives the stern draft to keel bottom. With 2m keel depth at stern:

ParameterValueDerivation
Total stern depth (keel bottom to waterline)7.86m15 cubits — Genesis 7:20
Stern keel depth2.00mDesign specification
Hull draft at stern5.86m7.86 − 2.00m
Stern trim~2.00mDesign target — see Section 3
Mean hull draft~4.86mStern hull draft − trim/2
Bow hull draft~3.86mMean − trim/2
Freeboard at stern9.86mD − stern hull draft = 15.72 − 5.86m
Freeboard at bow11.86mD − bow hull draft

Freeboard is very generous at both ends — no swamping risk even in extreme roll combined with 2m trim.

2. Displacement

At mean hull draft of 4.86m:

Displacement = ρ × L × B × T_mean × Cb
= 1.025 × 157.2 × 26.2 × 4.86 × 0.85
= ~17,400 tonnes

This is the total mass of everything on board — hull structure, cargo, animals, provisions, water, and ballast. All mass items must sum to 17,400 tonnes.

Note: If Genesis 7:20 refers to mean draft rather than stern draft, mean hull draft = 7.86 − 2.00 (keel) = 5.86m and displacement = ~21,000 tonnes. The stern-draft interpretation gives the lighter, more manageable vessel. This remains an open question pending the mass distribution calculation — if the natural mass (structure + cargo) comes in near 17,400 tonnes without forced ballasting, the stern-draft interpretation is confirmed.

3. Stern Trim — Design Rationale

A stern-heavy trim of ~2m is a deliberate design feature, not an accident of loading. It serves multiple functions:

FunctionMechanism
WeathervaningDeeper stern keel immersion → greater lateral resistance aft → stronger passive heading moment
Bow liftLight bow rises to oncoming seas → reduces slamming loads on bow structure
Following sea postureStern digs in, bow lifts and skids — optimal posture for weathervaning in following seas
Passive bilge drainageAll bilge water, wash-down water, and spilled animal water drains aft by gravity to moon pool complex
Moon pool efficiencyWaste flows to moon pool naturally — no pumping required
Keel taper confirmationBow keel taper (for weathervaning slip) starts in shallow draft zone — reinforces hydrodynamic asymmetry

Trim Calculation

Trim formula:  t = M_trim / (Δ × GML / L)

GML = BML + KB − KG

BML = IL / V  where IL = L³·B/12 (rectangular waterplane)
= 157.2³ × 26.2 / 12
= 8,440,000 m⁴

V = L × B × T_mean × Cb = 157.2 × 26.2 × 4.86 × 0.85 = 16,975 m³

BML = 8,440,000 / 16,975 = 497m

GML ≈ BML − (KG − KB) ≈ 497 − (KG − 2.4) ≈ ~495m  (KG effect negligible on GML)

For 2m trim:
M_trim = t × Δ × GML / L = 2 × 17,400 × 495 / 157.2 = ~109,500 tonne·m

Rock ballast at 40m aft of midships:
Mass required = 109,500 / 40 = ~2,740 tonnes of rock

2,740 tonnes of rock (granite, density 2,700 kg/m³) = 1,015 m³ — a layer approximately 290mm deep over the aft quarter of the hull footprint. Trivial to accommodate in the ballast zone.

Walking Comfort

2m trim over 157.2m = 1.3° trim angle. Imperceptible — people work comfortably on ski slopes of 10°+. Not a constraint.

4. Rock Ballast — The Universal Tuning Tool

Rock ballast is simultaneously the most effective and most flexible tuning variable in the design. It is dense (granite ~2,700 kg/m³ — nearly twice seawater), sits low, requires no maintenance, and was standard practice in all ancient vessels.

FunctionHow rock achieves it
Close mass budget to target displacementAdd rock until target draft reached
Tune KG downwardRock sits at ~0.5m height — pulls KG toward hull bottom
Set stern trimConcentrate rock aft of midships
Tune roll periodKG adjustment changes GM and hence roll period
Increase weathervaning momentAft-heavy trim deepens stern keel

Rock Ballast Geometry

4,000 tonnes of granite (density 2,700 kg/m³):

Volume = 4,000,000 / 2,700 = 1,480 m³

Over aft half of hull footprint (78.6m × 26.2m × 0.85 = 1,750 m²):
Layer depth = 1,480 / 1,750 = 0.85m — less than 1m deep

Or over full hull footprint (3,500 m²):
Layer depth = 1,480 / 3,500 = 0.42m — barely half a metre

Rock ballast is dimensionally trivial — a thin layer across the bilge achieves significant mass effect because of its high density. It sits naturally below the ballast water cells.

Key observations:

  • Header tanks at roof level (~15.5m) are the highest mass item — they raise KG. Their 260t at 15.5m = 4,030 t·m contribution is significant.
  • Rock ballast at 0.5m is the strongest KG depressant — every tonne of rock at 0.5m offsets many tonnes of cargo at 8–13m.
  • Animal mass is modest — total animal mass even at 50,000 animals averaging 50kg = 2,500 tonnes. Likely less given average kind size.
  • Provisions are light — dried/compressed food for 371 days, 8 people + animals. Probably 2,000–3,000 tonnes.

6. KG and GM — Provisional

KG cannot be calculated precisely until the hull structural mass is confirmed from the volume tally CAD model. The hull timber mass is the dominant item and its vertical CG determines the outcome.

Provisional bounds:

ScenarioKG (m)GM (m)Roll period (s)Character
Heavy rock ballast, light upper cargo~5.0~7.7~8sVery stiff — rough ride but very safe
Moderate rock, typical cargo distribution~7.0~5.7~9sStiff — uncomfortable but safe
Light rock, typical cargo~9.0~3.7~12sModerate — acceptable
Minimal rock, provisions high~11.0~1.7~17sComfortable but marginal survival margin

KM at mean hull draft 4.86m:

KB = 0.53 × T = 0.53 × 4.86 = 2.58m
BM = B² / (12 × T × Cb) = 26.2² / (12 × 4.86 × 0.85) = 686 / 49.6 = 13.83m
KM = KB + BM = 2.58 + 13.83 = 16.41m

Higher KM than previously calculated — shallower draft gives larger BM. This is favourable — more GM available for a given KG, or equivalently a higher KG can be tolerated for the same GM.

Design philosophy: A rough ride is preferable to capsizing. The GM target should be set by survival requirements in the worst-case Phase 3 sea state, not by comfort. Rock ballast is available in unlimited quantity and costs nothing — use it to achieve survival GM, accept the roll period consequence. The vegetation mat in Phases 1–2 provides comfort buffering during the period when seas are most confused.
