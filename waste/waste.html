Waste Management

Home › Waste Management

Waste management is the primary driver of crew workload. 8 people managing 6,000 animals over 371 days is only viable if waste collection is matched to animal metabolism — minimising handling effort while maintaining animal welfare. Three distinct zones handle different animal categories with different waste characters.

1. The Three-Zone System

The fundamental design principle: match the collection method to the waste character. Never collect what doesn't need collecting. Never use a complex system where a simple one suffices.

ZoneAnimalsWaste characterMethodFrequency
Zone 1
Never collect		 Reptiles, small dry birds (corvids, doves, small passerines), flightless birds (small) Low moisture, low volume per animal. Desiccates rapidly in warm enclosed environment. Mesh cage floors — droppings fall through, desiccate in subfloor litter layer. Self-managing. Subfloor cleanout every ~60 days
Zone 2
Bi-monthly cleanout		 Small mammals (rodents, rabbits, small marsupials), medium mammals Accumulated litter — bedding + droppings. Manageable in bulk. Low moisture relative to Zone 3. Sawdust/straw litter. Shovelled to barrows every ~60 days. Wheeled to moon pool zone for disposal. Every 60 days — ~6 cleanouts per voyage
Zone 3
Wash-down		 Large animals (bovines, equines, large deer), large flightless birds (ostriches, cassowaries) High volume, high moisture. Continuous production. Cannot accumulate for 60 days. Slatted/grated floor over shallow collection channel. Gravity drain to moon pool via short sewer run. Periodic wash-down with water. Daily wash-down — 2 crew, ~2 hours
Promenade bird zone: Flying birds in atrium cages above 2m height deposit droppings by gravity onto the planting strip below — a closed-loop system that converts waste directly to fertiliser. Not collected, not disposed — immediately useful. See Passive Systems.

2. Zone Locations and Deck Assignment

ZoneDeckRationale
Zone 1 — reptiles/dry birds Deck 1 (Lowers) and Deck 2 (Twos) Reptiles prefer dark and cool — Deck 1 natural. Minimal drainage infrastructure needed. Zone 1 subfloor is essentially dead space — minimal depth required.
Zone 2 — small/medium mammals Deck 2 (Twos) primarily Middle position. Barrow route to moon pool via ramp — manageable gradient. Bi-monthly schedule means crew can plan the cleanout efficiently.
Zone 3 — large animals Deck 3 (Threes — top deck) Adjacent to moon pools which drain downward through the hull. Short sewer run — only a few metres horizontally to moon pool, then gravity down. Best ventilation for highest-output animals. Wash-down water drains by gravity.

The bi-monthly cleanout schedule (6 times per voyage) is the most labour-intensive event — approximately one full day for 3 crew members. Planned in advance, this represents a manageable periodic maintenance event rather than a continuous burden. The remaining 59 days between cleanouts require only the Zone 3 daily wash-down for waste management.

5. Waste Quantities

Waste production estimates (torpor-reduced metabolic rates): Zone 1 — reptiles/dry birds (~3,000 animals): Typical output: ~5g dry waste/animal/day Total: 3,000 × 0.005kg × 60 days = ~900kg per cleanout cycle Desiccated volume: ~1.5 m³ per cleanout ✓ manageable Zone 2 — small/medium mammals (~2,500 animals): Typical output: ~20g wet waste/animal/day (torpor reduced) Total: 2,500 × 0.020kg × 60 days = ~3,000kg per cleanout Volume with bedding: ~10 m³ per cleanout Barrow loads at 100L per barrow: ~100 barrow loads per cleanout 2 crew × 1 day: ~50 barrow loads each — demanding but achievable ✓ Zone 3 — large animals (~500 animals): Typical output: ~5kg wet waste/animal/day (reduced) Daily total: 500 × 5kg = 2,500kg/day = 2.5 tonnes/day Daily volume: ~2.5 m³/day — flushed directly to moon pool Annual total: ~900 tonnes — all gravity drained ✓
Zone 3 daily volume is significant — 2.5 m³/day of large animal waste must drain reliably to the moon pool every day for 371 days. This confirms the short sewer run requirement: Zone 3 must be adjacent to the moon pool, not at the far end of the vessel. See Moon Pool for drainage design.

6. Closed Loop — Bird Guano to Garden

The promenade bird zone creates the only complete closed waste loop on the vessel:

Bird guano nutrient cycle: ~500 birds in atrium cages above 2m Guano falls by gravity to planting strip below Planting strip: ~1m wide × 125.4m × 3 decks = ~376 m² (partial occupation) Bird guano nitrogen content: ~15% (one of richest natural fertilisers) Daily guano production: ~500 birds × 5g/day = 2.5kg/day Annual: ~926kg of high-nitrogen fertiliser continuously applied Sprout/vegetable yield from 376m² with continuous guano fertilisation: Supports significant fresh food supplement — see Passive Systems page

This is the only waste stream that is not disposed of but directly utilised. It requires zero crew intervention — gravity does the collection, the plants do the processing. An elegant closed loop from the first principles of the design.

7. Waste and Ventilation Interaction

Waste management and ventilation are closely linked — poor waste management produces gases that overwhelm the ventilation system; good ventilation accelerates waste desiccation in Zone 1 reducing management burden:

InteractionEffect
Stack effect draws ammonia upwardZone 1 and 2 ammonia naturally vented through atrium to ṣohar — reduces toxic accumulation
Warm dry air accelerates Zone 1 desiccationFaster desiccation = less odour, less mass to remove at cleanout
Cedar aromatics counteract waste odoursContinuous passive odour management throughout voyage
Zone 3 wash-down water adds humidityPartially offset by stack effect removing humid air — managed by ventilation design
Methane from anaerobic fermentationLighter than air — rises naturally to ṣohar exhaust. Fire risk if concentrated — ventilation prevents accumulation.
Methane fire risk: Large animal manure produces methane under anaerobic conditions. The Zone 3 daily wash-down prevents anaerobic accumulation by keeping the collection channel flushed. The stack effect prevents methane concentration in any zone. The ṣohar provides continuous exhaust. Fire risk is managed by design rather than requiring active monitoring.
