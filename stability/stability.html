1. Design Philosophy — Setting GM from Seakeeping

Stability design proceeds top-down: set the target roll period first, derive the required GM, then calculate the required KG and arrange masses to achieve it. This avoids the trap of calculating GM from an assumed mass distribution and accepting whatever roll period results.

The Ark is a passive vessel with no propulsion, no ability to alter heading under power, and 8 crew managing ~6,000 animals over 371 days. Animal welfare and crew safety both demand comfortable motion. Snappy roll (short period, high GM) causes stress, injury, feed/water spillage and structural fatigue. The design target is a long, gentle roll period.

Non-linear stability objective: The ideal GZ curve is shallow at small angles (soft, comfortable motion) and steepening at larger angles (strong capsize resistance). This is achieved by the combination of: curved bilge (increasing waterplane with heel), outboard bilge keels (engaging at larger angles), and corner header tanks (outboard high mass). See Seakeeping for the wave environment context.

2. Target Roll Period

The flood wave environment is dominated by confused crossing seas from multiple directions simultaneously — see Seakeeping. No single dominant wave period exists throughout most of the voyage, reducing resonance risk. The receding phase (days 150–371) introduces a directional wind sea from God's drying wind (Genesis 8:1) with period approximately 14–18 seconds for a sustained global wind.

Roll periodAssessment
<10 secondsToo stiff — snappy violent roll, animal stress, structural fatigue
10–15 secondsMarginal — uncomfortable, close to wave period range
20–25 secondsTarget — comfortable, well clear of dominant wave periods
>30 secondsVery tender — risk of slow progressive heel

Selected target: T = 22 seconds — midpoint of comfort range.

3. Required GM

Roll Period Formula

T = 2π × k / √(g × GM) Where: T = roll period (seconds) k = radius of gyration (m) — accounts for mass distribution g = 9.81 m/s² GM = metacentric height (m) Rearranging for GM: GM = (2π × k)² / (g × T²)

Radius of Gyration (k)

k = C × B where C is typically 0.39–0.45 for conventional vessels. For the Ark, mass is concentrated outboard (corner tanks, heavy hull skin, outboard keels) giving a higher C than normal. Using C = 0.45:

k = 0.45 × 22.4m (internal beam) = ~10.1m Note: outboard bilge keels and corner header tanks increase effective k further. This figure is conservative — actual k likely higher, meaning actual required GM is lower than calculated here, easing the KG requirement.

GM Calculation

At T = 22 seconds, k = 10.1m: GM = (2π × 10.1)² / (9.81 × 22²) = (63.5)² / (9.81 × 484) = 4,032 / 4,748 = ~0.85m Target GM = 0.85m

4. Hull Geometry at Target Draft

Draft — Genesis 7:20

"The waters rose and covered the mountains to a depth of 15 cubits." — Genesis 7:20

The standard interpretation: water depth above the highest mountains was 15 cubits. The engineering interpretation: Noah reported minimum clearance above the mountains equal to his known draft of 15 cubits — the only depth measurement he could actually make. Noah had no mechanism to measure water depth above submerged mountain peaks, but knew his draft precisely — it was a fixed design parameter.

KB — Centre of Buoyancy Height

For a wall-sided hull with rounded bilge:

KB ≈ 0.53 × draft = 0.53 × 7.77m = ~4.12m

BM — Metacentric Radius

BM = I_waterplane / V_displaced Waterplane second moment of area: I = (1/12) × L × B³ × Cw = (1/12) × 155.4 × 22.4³ × 0.88 = ~127,000 m⁴ Displacement volume: V = 20,825t / 1.025 = ~20,317 m³ BM = 127,000 / 20,317 = ~6.25m

KM — Metacentre Height

KM = KB + BM = 4.12 + 6.25 = ~10.37m

5. Required KG

GM = KM - KG KG = KM - GM = 10.37 - 0.85 = ~9.52m

KG = 9.52m on a 15.54m hull — the centre of gravity must sit at ~61% of hull height, which is above the waterline (7.77m draft). This is unusual but not unprecedented — sailing yachts regularly have KG above waterline due to rig and sails. Here it is achieved by mass distribution, not a rig.

KG is very sensitive to k: If outboard keels and corner tanks push k from 10.1m to say 11m, the required GM drops to ~0.71m and required KG drops to ~9.66m. The outboard keel specification (pending) will refine this significantly. A higher k is better — it allows a lower (more achievable) KG for the same roll period.

6. Achieving the Target KG

KG ~9.5m requires mass to be concentrated HIGH in the vessel. The natural tendency of a ship is to have KG well below this — heavy structure low, cargo in the hold. The Ark requires deliberate high mass placement.

7. Outboard Keel Contribution

The Ark has triple bilge keels — the specification is pending but their effect on k can be estimated. If the keels add say 500 tonnes at a radius of 11m from centreline:

Additional I from keels: ΔI = 500,000 kg × (11m)² × 2 sides = 121,000,000 kg·m² Current I (from hull mass distribution): I_current = m × k² = 20,311,000 × 10.1² = ~2,070,000,000 kg·m² New total I: I_new = 2,070,000,000 + 121,000,000 = ~2,191,000,000 kg·m² New k: k_new = √(I_new / m_total) = √(2,191,000,000 / 20,811,000) = ~10.26m New required GM at T=22s: GM = (2π × 10.26)² / (9.81 × 484) = ~0.88m (barely changes) New required KG: KG = 10.37 - 0.88 = ~9.49m

The keel mass alone doesn't close the gap — the keels contribute to roll DAMPING (resisting roll velocity) more than to the period itself. The period is dominated by GM. The real benefit of outboard keels is damping amplitude, not changing period.

Outstanding question: How to close the 2.33m KG gap. Options being evaluated: reduce ballast fill + move fodder upward + stone on weatherdeck. Full sensitivity analysis pending CAD mass model. Outboard keel specification needed.

8. Ballast Tuning

The ballast zone (~4,750 m³ capacity) provides active trim and stability control. Reducing ballast fill raises KG (removes low mass) and reduces draft simultaneously.

Effect of reducing ballast from 1,500t to 500t (removing 1,000t at ~1.0m height): Moment removed: 1,000 × 1.0 = 1,000 t·m Mass removed: 1,000t New total mass: 20,311 − 1,000 = 19,311t New total moment: 146,097 − 1,000 = 145,097 t·m New KG: 145,097 / 19,311 = ~7.51m KG rises from 7.19m to 7.51m — improvement of 0.32m Still well short of 9.52m target. Effect on draft: New displacement: 19,311t New draft ≈ 7.77 × (19,311/20,311) = ~7.39m — slightly less than target

Ballast reduction alone is insufficient to close the KG gap. Multiple measures needed simultaneously.
