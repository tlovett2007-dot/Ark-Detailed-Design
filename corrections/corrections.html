The Ark Encounter (AiG, Williamstown KY, opened 2016) was developed partly from the author's prior CAD work and design concepts. During the artistic and exhibition development process, a number of engineering decisions were modified — in some cases replacing passive, self-regulating systems with more complex alternatives that introduce unnecessary failure modes. This page documents those modifications, the original engineering rationale, and the correct solution.

Note on tone: These corrections are made in the spirit of engineering rigour, not personal criticism. Exhibition design involves legitimate constraints (visual appeal, visitor understanding, construction practicality) that differ from genuine naval architecture requirements. The corrections documented here are relevant to the feasibility argument — what Noah's vessel actually required — not to the validity of the Ark Encounter as a museum experience.

1. Animal Water Delivery — Rotating Vessel vs Valve

Original Design (Author)

A rotating vessel water delivery system — the ancient and proven chicken drinker principle:

ElementDescription
VesselInverted container filled with water, open at base
TroughShallow trough below the vessel opening
MechanismAnimal drinks from trough → water level drops → air enters vessel → water flows by gravity to restore level → cycle repeats
Moving partsNone — gravity and atmospheric pressure only
Failure modesNone beyond physical damage to vessel
Crew operationNone — refill vessel periodically from header tank above
Historical precedentChicken drinker design used continuously for centuries — proven reliable at scale
Vessel motion toleranceExcellent — system self-regulates regardless of orientation within normal roll angles

Ark Encounter Modification

The rotating vessel was replaced with a stationary drum with a valve controlling water flow — apparently for visual clarity in the exhibition context (attributed to artistic direction, possibly Travis Wilson).

Engineering Problem with the Valve System

The valve system introduces a critical failure mode absent from the original design:

Failure rate analysis: Animals on Ark: ~6,000 kinds × 2 = ~12,000 animals Water valves required: ~12,000 (one per cage/drinker) Voyage duration: 371 days Valve operations: 12,000 × ~4 operations/day × 371 days = ~17,800,000 At 6-Sigma quality (3.4 defects per million opportunities): Expected failures: 17,800,000 × 0.0000034 = ~61 valve failures At more realistic manufacturing quality (4-Sigma = 6,210 defects/million): Expected failures: 17,800,000 × 0.00621 = ~110,538 valve failures 😄 Even at 6-Sigma: 61 failures × potential animal kind loss = feasibility risk Pre-industrial bronze/timber valves: far below 6-Sigma

A single leaking valve that allows water to drain from a cage, or a stuck valve that prevents water reaching an animal, could kill the occupants. With thousands of valves operating for 371 days in a pre-industrial timber and bronze construction, the probability of at least one failure approaches certainty.

The rotating vessel system has no valves. It cannot fail through valve malfunction. The only failure mode is physical damage to the vessel itself — visible, immediately obvious, and trivially repaired with pitched fabric and a replacement vessel.

The Correct System

PropertyRotating vessel (original)Valve system (AE)
Moving partsNone ✓Valve mechanism ✗
Failure modesPhysical damage only ✓Valve leak, valve stuck, valve corrosion ✗
Crew operationPeriodic refill only ✓Valve inspection/maintenance ✗
Vessel motion toleranceExcellent ✓Dependent on valve orientation ✗
Pre-industrial feasibilityProven technology ✓Requires precision manufacture ✗
Self-regulatingYes — animal controls its own supply ✓No — valve must be correctly set ✗
Engineering principle: In a system where failure = potential extinction of an animal kind, passive self-regulating systems with zero moving parts are always preferred over active systems requiring precision components. The chicken drinker principle eliminates the failure mode entirely rather than reducing its probability. Noah had 8 people to manage 6,000 animals — passive systems are not a convenience, they are a necessity.

2. Hydroponic Plant Stand — Static Test on a Ship

The Ark Encounter System

A hydroponic plant stand with a shallow-angle gravity-fed watering channel — water flows along channels at a slight gradient, delivering moisture to plant roots by gravity flow. Tested with a marble on a stationary floor to confirm the gradient was sufficient for flow. Test result: marble rolled along channel. ✓

The Engineering Problem

The test was conducted on a stationary floor. The Ark is not stationary.

Effect of vessel roll on shallow-angle watering channel: Design gradient: say 2° (shallow angle for slow water flow) Vessel roll angle: 8° at sea state 5 (moderate conditions) Roll WITH channel gradient: Effective slope = 2° + 8° = 10° — water rushes rapidly to low end Plants at high end: starved Plants at low end: drowned Roll AGAINST channel gradient: Effective slope = 2° - 8° = -6° — water flows BACKWARD Plants at high end: drowned (water reverses up channel) Plants at low end: starved At fully developed sea state (8° roll, period ~22 seconds): Channel alternately floods and drains every 11 seconds No plant survives this treatment for 371 days 😄

The Marble Test Problem

Testing a gravity-fed system with a marble on a stationary floor confirms only that the gradient exists. It says nothing about system behaviour when the floor tilts ±8° every 22 seconds for 371 days. This is a fundamental test methodology error — the test environment does not represent the operating environment.

Correct Solutions

SystemVessel motion toleranceNotes
Wicking/capillary systemExcellent — capillary action independent of orientationProven technology, simple construction, passive
Individual pot trays with drainageGood — each pot self-containedWater retained in pot regardless of vessel angle
Deep substrate bedsGood — soil retains moisture through roll cyclesThe promenade planting strip already specified this way
Vertical tower growingModerate — depends on orientationNeeds motion analysis
Shallow gravity channelPoor ✗AE system — fails in any seaway
The promenade planting strip in this design uses deep substrate beds fertilised by bird guano from above — a wicking/soil-retention system with no gravity channels. Vessel motion irrelevant to plant survival. The sprouting tray system uses shallow trays that are self-contained — each tray retains its moisture through roll cycles independently.
